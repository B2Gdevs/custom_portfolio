# Letter III — The child

Mother,

I am carrying Owain’s child.

I have known for a fortnight — sickness in the mornings, breasts tender, a secret pressed under my ribs that should have been pure joy. I imagined writing you this letter with a different ink: *Grandmother*, *names we might choose*, *when I might come south for the lying-in if the roads are safe.* I imagined your hands on my belly and your voice telling me old Hallinghorn charms that are half prayer and half habit.

Instead I write you from a kitchen that feels like a courtroom.

They brought a girl into the house.

Not a cousin from a branch of the family Owain forgot to mention. Not a debt paid in flesh that was explained to me in sentences I could repeat without shame. A child **Jack** names as his — his blood, his mistake, his story — wrapped in words that sound like mercy if you say them fast enough. Owain speaks of duty. He speaks of the Patron’s honor, of not letting an infant pay for men’s sins. He speaks as if our roof were a charity hall and I were a nun who had taken vows to every stray the wind blew in.

I asked *why now*.

I asked *why us*.

I asked why, when my own body is already building his heir, we needed another mouth — *just because*, Mother, as if hunger were a virtue and my womb were not proof enough that this family was *already* growing.

Owain looked at me with tired kindness, the kind that hurts worse than shouting, and said we had room. We had room. As if room were only boards and bread and not air, not precedence, not the thin daily respect a wife trades for peace.

I am furious until I shake. At night I lie awake and rehearse speeches I will never deliver in daylight because a woman who complains about a bastard child is always the villain in the story, never the person who was asked to swallow stone.

By day I smile at the girl. I call her nothing cruel with my mouth. I teach my face the expressions the church praises — patience, mercy, the soft mouth of a woman who understands that the world is hard.

Her name is Enid.

I hate that I know it. I hate that I have to use it. I hate that when she looks at me with a child’s open face, part of me wants to be fair, and fairness feels like betrayal of the child *inside* me who should have been first in every eye.

Write to me. Tell me I am not mad. Or tell me I am mad and I will at least know the shape of it.

**Tanya**
